# Construction Brief: Congressional Constituent Services Letter

This is not a fill-in-the-blank template. It is a brief for constructing a letter from the user to their congressional representatives — most often House representative and both Senators — requesting the constituent-services intervention that congressional offices provide for their district residents.

> ⚠ **Before this is sent:** the user is asserting facts that will be read by congressional staff and may be referenced in subsequent communications between the office and the platform. Verify each claim against evidence first.

---

## What this document needs to do

A congressional constituent-services letter serves a specific narrow purpose: cause a constituent-services staffer in the user's congressional office to send an inquiry to the platform's government-affairs team on the user's behalf.

This is not a request for legislation. It is not a request for the member's personal attention. It is a request for a service that every congressional office provides — assistance to constituents navigating disputes with federal agencies, large corporations, and (increasingly) major tech platforms. Every office has a constituent-services team that does this work routinely.

The audience is a constituent-services staffer (often a recent college graduate). They process dozens of similar requests a week. They are sympathetic, time-constrained, and looking for cases that:

1. **Are clearly within the constituent**'s actual residence and the office's district
2. **Have a documented history of platform inaction**, not just one frustrating support ticket
3. **Have a specific actionable inquiry** the office can send to the platform

A letter that satisfies these gets the inquiry sent. The inquiry gets routed through the platform's government-affairs office (a high-priority queue) and often produces a response within 1–3 weeks. Even when it doesn't directly resolve the case, it adds another regulatory-pressure data point.

---

## What always goes in

1. **Constituent identification.** User's full legal name, full residential address (must be in the district / state), phone, email. The address establishes constituency — without it, the office can't act.
2. **Recipient identification.** Member's name, office address, and (importantly) the address of the *district office* if applicable. District offices typically handle constituent services; DC offices handle policy. Address to both for House members; address to one of the state offices for Senators.
3. **A brief case summary.** 4–6 sentences. What account, what platform, what action was taken, when, what the user has already tried, what's outstanding. Plain language, no legal terminology.
4. **A clear constituent-services ask.** "I am writing to respectfully request that your constituent services office assist me by submitting an inquiry to [Platform legal entity]'s government affairs office regarding my pending case." Specific, actionable, within the office's normal scope.
5. **A summary of regulatory filings already made.** State AG complaint number, FTC complaint number, BBB case if applicable. This signals the user has done the work — the congressional office is not being asked to substitute for ordinary remedies, but to add weight to a worked case.
6. **A documentary request.** "I would be happy to provide my full documentation package upon request." Don't attach the full package upfront; constituent-services staff often can't process large attachments. Offer it on request.
7. **The user's signature, contact info, and an authorization statement** — congressional offices typically need a signed Privacy Act release before contacting the platform on the constituent's behalf. The letter notes willingness to sign whatever release the office sends.

---

## What varies by case

### Cases involving small business or income loss

Lead with the income/business dimension. Constituent-services offices prioritize cases involving constituents' livelihoods. "I am a [profession] in [city] whose primary income source has been disabled by [Platform]." This positioning gets faster response than "I lost my social media account."

### Cases involving public-interest dimensions

If the user is a journalist, a public official, an advocacy organization, or has a documented audience that depends on the account, surface that briefly. Congressional offices respond faster to cases with public-interest implications. Don't overstate; do mention if accurate.

### Cases involving platform pattern issues

If the user's case appears to be part of a pattern (e.g., similar accounts being targeted, a documented cluster of false reports, a known platform algorithm issue), reference it briefly. "Other constituents in the district may be experiencing similar issues" — but only if true and supportable.

### Cases for senators vs. House members

House offices typically have closer constituent ties and respond faster on individual cases. Senate offices have larger staffs and process more inquiries, often more slowly. File with both. Address each letter to the relevant office with appropriate honorifics ("The Honorable [Name]") and address.

---

## What good looks like

A representative letter is 350–500 words. Long enough to convey the case clearly, short enough that a busy staffer reads it in under 2 minutes.

A representative opening:

> The Honorable [Name]
> [Office Address]
>
> Re: Constituent Services Request — Account Recovery, [Platform]
>
> Dear [Representative/Senator] [Last Name]:
>
> I am writing as a constituent of [district / state] to respectfully request your office's assistance with a pending matter involving [Platform]. My [account / business / professional channel] on [Platform] has been [disabled / hijacked / locked / charged without authorization] since [date], affecting [income / professional reach / community of N people]. I have exhausted [Platform]'s standard support channels and have filed parallel complaints with the [state] Attorney General [#reference], the Federal Trade Commission [#reference], and [BBB if applicable]. To date, [Platform] has not provided a substantive response.

Then 2–3 paragraphs unpacking the case, the impact, and the specific inquiry the office is being asked to send. Then the signature.

The tone is respectful but not deferential. The user is a constituent, not a supplicant.

---

## What to avoid

- **Asking for legislation.** Constituent services is operational, not policy. "I want laws passed about big tech" is the wrong frame and gets the letter routed to a policy staffer who can't help.
- **Personal appeals to the member.** Members rarely read constituent letters personally. The audience is staff. Don't write as if the member will read it.
- **Highlighting partisan or political positions.** The case is about consumer protection, which is bipartisan. Mentioning the user's political views (positive or negative) is irrelevant and counterproductive.
- **Length over 1 page.** Staffers won't read a 2-page letter. Tighten.
- **Naming legal theories.** Same constraint as everywhere else.
- **Filing with members outside the user's district / state.** Constituent services only acts on actual constituents. The user must file with their own representatives.

---

## Required structural elements

- **Letterhead-style opening.** Member's name, office address, salutation.
- **Subject line.** "Re: Constituent Services Request — [brief description]"
- **Opening that establishes constituency** (the user's residential address, signaling they live in the district).
- **Case summary** (4–6 sentences).
- **Regulatory filings summary** (1–2 sentences).
- **Specific constituent-services ask.**
- **Documentation availability statement.**
- **Privacy Act release willingness statement** (offices typically need a release before contacting the platform).
- **Closing and full signature block** (name, residential address, phone, email).

---

## Outline-first protocol (REQUIRED for this document)

Before writing the letter, produce a structured outline:

1. **The case-summary sentence** — one sentence the staffer could quote when describing the case to a supervisor.
2. **The most district-relevant fact** (constituent's profession, the local business affected, etc.) — what makes this a constituent-services case rather than a generic complaint.
3. **The exact inquiry the office is being asked to send** — drafted as a one-sentence ask the staffer could paste into their template.
4. **The 3 supporting documents the office is most likely to want** — from the user's documentation package.
5. **The single sentence in the planned letter most likely to be questioned** by the staffer — flagged so the user can verify it before sending.

Present the outline to the user. Get confirmation. Then write the prose.

---

## After the user sends the letter

Walk them through:

1. **Find the right office.** House.gov or Senate.gov — search by zip code for the user's House representative; both senators are state-wide. Use the district office address for House; one of the state offices for Senate.
2. **Send via certified mail.** Email is also accepted but certified mail produces a delivery receipt. Both methods are common; certified mail is recommended for higher-stakes cases.
3. **Expect a Privacy Act release form.** Congressional offices typically respond first with a release form for the user to sign before they'll contact the platform on the constituent's behalf. Sign and return promptly.
4. **Wait 14–30 days.** Constituent services typically takes 1–3 weeks to send the inquiry, then another 1–3 weeks for the platform to respond. The office usually shares the platform's response with the constituent.
5. **Save all correspondence.** Office acknowledgment, release form receipts, the inquiry text (if shared), the platform's response. All of it is evidence for subsequent stages.

# Construction Brief: Internal Reconsideration Reply

This is not a fill-in-the-blank template. It is a brief for constructing a reply to the user's existing platform support ticket — the Stage 1 step that exhausts the platform's own internal escalation paths before regulatory pressure begins.

> ⚠ **Before this is sent:** the user is asserting facts to platform support staff. Verify each factual claim against their evidence before sending. Internal reconsideration content tends to get re-quoted in subsequent platform automated responses, so weaknesses propagate.

---

## What this document needs to do

A reconsideration reply is a single message sent into the user's *existing* support ticket (NOT a new ticket — opening a new ticket resets the queue position and erases history). Its job is to push the case from automated-tier review to a human-tier review with authority to act, and to produce a documented refusal that becomes evidence in Stage 3.

Two outcomes are equally valuable:

1. **The platform reviews and acts.** Some cases resolve at this step.
2. **The platform refuses or auto-replies again.** This documented non-response is exactly what subsequent state AG complaints cite. Either outcome moves the case forward.

The audience is a support tier-1 or tier-2 reviewer who reads the message in 30–90 seconds and decides whether to escalate it internally. They are not lawyers, not policy specialists. The message must be brief, calm, and immediately legible.

---

## What always goes in

1. **The existing ticket reference.** Subject line and opening sentence reference the existing ticket number explicitly. "Re: Ticket #[number] — Reconsideration Request." Never open a new ticket.
2. **A one-paragraph case summary.** What account, what action was taken, when. Five sentences max.
3. **What the user has provided previously and what remains unaddressed.** Specific to this ticket — "I provided [X] on [date], which has not been substantively reviewed."
4. **Any new evidence or context.** If the user has additional documentation since the original report, surface it here.
5. **A specific, actionable request.** "I respectfully request that this case be escalated to a tier-2 review with attention to the [specific evidence] I previously provided."
6. **Calm, professional tone throughout.** The reviewer is a person with discretion. They escalate cases that read as legitimate and sympathetic; they don't escalate cases that read as combative.

---

## What varies by case

### Hijacked accounts (Scenario A)

Emphasize ongoing third-party harm. "The hijacker continues to [send unsolicited messages / make purchases / impersonate me]. Each day of inaction increases the harm to me and my followers." This is genuine urgency, not threat — and it gives the reviewer a reason to escalate now rather than later.

### Banned accounts where the user did not commit the violation (Scenario B)

Be specific about the platform's stated reason and equally specific about why it's incorrect. "The platform action cited [stated reason]. I did not [the alleged conduct]. The evidence I previously provided shows [what the evidence actually shows]." Avoid sounding aggrieved; sound like someone correcting a factual error.

### Cases with retracted third-party reports

This is the strongest reconsideration posture and should be led with: "The original [reporting party] has withdrawn the report on [date]. Confirmation attached. Despite this, the platform action remains in place. I respectfully request immediate reconsideration in light of the retraction."

### Lockout cases (Scenario C)

When there is no stated reason, the reconsideration becomes a request for explanation. "I am unable to access my account and have not been provided a reason. I have submitted the standard verification materials [list]. Please advise of any specific issue I can address, or of the path to substantive review."

### Billing cases (Scenario D)

Surface the dollar amount and the parallel chargeback path. "I have been charged $[amount] for transactions I did not authorize. I have initiated a chargeback through my issuing bank [if applicable]. I am also requesting that [Platform] review and reverse the charges directly. Please advise of your dispute resolution timeline."

### Impersonation cases (Scenario E)

Make the impersonator's account the focus, not the user's. "Account [@impersonator-handle] is impersonating me. I have provided proof of my identity [list]. Despite [number] of impersonation reports, the impersonator account remains active. I respectfully request that the impersonator account be reviewed against the platform's published impersonation policy."

## What good looks like

A reconsideration reply is short — 200–400 words. The reviewer's attention is the constraint. A 2,000-word essay gets skimmed; a 300-word focused message gets read.

A representative opening:

> Re: Ticket #[number] — Reconsideration Request
>
> Hello,
>
> I am writing to request reconsideration of my [Platform] account [@username] under the existing ticket above. I previously provided [evidence type] on [date]. The most recent reply I received was [date], indicating [substance]. I would like to surface two facts for review.

Then 2–3 short paragraphs of facts and request. Then a sign-off.

---

## What to avoid

- **Opening a new ticket.** Always reply within the existing ticket. New tickets reset the queue and destroy continuity.
- **Naming legal theories or threatening regulatory escalation in this stage.** Stage 1 is the inside game. Threats here mark the user as adversarial and route the ticket to legal/compliance review (which delays response). Save regulatory framing for the Stage 1.5 legal notice.
- **Length.** Anything over 600 words gets skimmed. Tighten.
- **Capital letters, exclamation points, or escalating emotional language.** All read as demanding-customer signals. Keep the register flat and professional.
- **Repeating evidence the platform already has.** Reference what was previously sent ("I previously provided X on [date]"), don't re-paste it.
- **Vague requests.** "Please help me" — the reviewer doesn't know how. "Please escalate this case to tier-2 review" — actionable.

---

## Required structural elements

- **Subject line.** "Re: Ticket #[number] — Reconsideration Request" (or similar — references existing ticket).
- **Opening reference to existing ticket number.**
- **Brief factual summary.**
- **What's already been provided and what remains unaddressed.**
- **Specific request for action (escalation, review, restoration, explanation).**
- **Sign-off with full name and any prior account verification details (e.g., last 4 of card on file, account creation year).**

---

## Outline-first protocol (OPTIONAL for this document)

Outline-first is recommended but not required for reconsideration replies. The stakes are lower than AG complaints and the document is short enough that drafting prose directly is reasonable. However, for compromised accounts with ongoing third-party harm or for cases where the user has had multiple prior failed reconsideration attempts, run the outline step:

1. **The single most-actionable request** the reviewer could grant
2. **The single piece of evidence** most directly supporting that request
3. **The platform's most likely refusal** and how the message preempts it

Then write prose.

---

## After the user sends the reconsideration reply

Walk them through:

1. **Wait 7–14 days.** Reconsideration replies typically get a response in this window. An automated re-rejection is acceptable as Stage 1 exhaustion; a substantive review is rare but possible.
2. **If the response is automated re-rejection:** save it. It's evidence in Stage 3 filings. Move to the legal-notice step (Stage 1.5).
3. **If there is no response after 14 days:** also acceptable as Stage 1 exhaustion. Move to legal-notice with a "no response received" framing.
4. **If the response is substantive:** evaluate carefully. A genuine resolution stops the escalation. A delay tactic ("we are reviewing your case, please allow 30–60 days") does not — proceed to legal-notice and reference the delay tactic in subsequent filings.

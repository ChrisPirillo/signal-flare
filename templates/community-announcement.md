# Construction Brief: Community / Audience Announcement

This is not a fill-in-the-blank template. It is a brief for constructing a public message from the user to their existing audience or community, alerting them that the user has lost access to a particular account and providing alternative contact channels.

> ⚠ **This is the one document in the skill that's voice-driven, not procedural.** It goes to the user's audience, not to a regulator. The user's voice — how they normally talk to their followers, customers, or community — matters more than any structural pattern. Help the user write in their own voice; do not impose a corporate or legalistic register.

---

## What this document needs to do

A community announcement serves several purposes simultaneously:

1. **Protect the audience from impersonation or fraud** by the party currently in control of the account (Scenarios A and E especially)
2. **Preserve the user's audience and reach** during the recovery window — directing followers to alternative channels reduces audience attrition
3. **Establish provenance** — a public, dated announcement that the user lost access on a specific date is itself evidence in subsequent regulatory filings
4. **Reduce confusion** — give the user's existing audience clarity about what happened

The audience is the user's own community — the people who follow, subscribe to, customer-with, or work with the user. They are sympathetic by default. They want clarity, not legal posture.

---

## What always goes in

1. **A clear factual statement of what happened.** "On [date], I lost access to my [Platform] account [@username]. I am working to recover it."
2. **What the audience should NOT do.** "Anyone currently posting from that account is not me. Do not click links, send money, or respond to direct messages from that account."
3. **Where to reach the user instead.** Alternative channels — other social platforms, an email address, a Patreon, a website. List 1–3 alternatives, not 10.
4. **A request for help if appropriate.** "If you've received messages from the account since [date], please screenshot and send them to [contact] — I'm building documentation."
5. **An expected timeline if known.** Don't promise a date, but acknowledge it may take weeks. "I am working through the proper channels and expect this to take several weeks."

---

## What varies by scenario

### Hijacked accounts (Scenario A) — most urgent posture

Lead with the warning. The hijacker is actively in the account and may be defrauding the user's audience. Don't spend a paragraph contextualizing — get to the protective information immediately. "URGENT: my [Platform] account was hijacked on [date]. Anyone receiving messages, donation requests, or links from that account is not me. Do not click links or send anything. Real me is at: [alternative channels]."

### Banned / disabled accounts (Scenario B and C)

Less urgent — the account is dark, not weaponized. Tone can be calmer and more reflective. Emphasize provenance ("I want to document on the record what happened") and the alternative channels.

### Impersonation (Scenario E)

Different from hijacking — the user still has their original account, but a copycat account is impersonating them. The announcement goes on the user's *original* account. "There is an impersonator account [@impersonator-handle] currently active. That is not me. My only [Platform] account is [@real-handle]. The impersonator has already [done what they've done]; I am working with [Platform] to have it removed."

### Billing cases (Scenario D)

Community announcement is rarely needed for billing cases — those are typically private between the user and the platform. Skip unless the billing issue has somehow gone public (e.g., the user has been falsely accused of fraud and needs to address it).

## What good looks like

This document varies more by user voice than any other in the skill. A creator with a casual chatty voice writes a casual chatty announcement. A small business owner with a professional voice writes a professional announcement. An academic writes academically. The user's audience knows their voice; suddenly speaking in a different register is its own credibility damage.

Two examples for the same scenario, two different voices:

**A casual creator:**

> hey friends - bad news. my main account got hijacked yesterday afternoon. someone got into my email through a phishing link i clicked (extremely embarrassing, learn from my mistakes). the account is in their hands right now, i can't get back in.
>
> heads up: anyone DMing you from my account or asking you to click stuff is NOT ME. especially anything about crypto or "investment opportunities" — that's the scammer. please don't click and please don't send anything.
>
> i'm at [alt account] for now and you can email me at [email]. trying to get this fixed through proper channels, hoping it's resolved within a couple weeks but no promises.
>
> if you got a weird message from "me" since yesterday, send me a screenshot if you can — i'm collecting documentation. thanks for the patience, this sucks.

**A professional services provider:**

> Important update for clients and contacts:
>
> On April 28, my professional [Platform] account was compromised. I am working through the proper recovery channels and have filed reports with the platform and with relevant regulators. The recovery process typically takes 3–6 weeks.
>
> During this period: anyone receiving messages, requests, or direct contact from the @[handle] account is not me. Please do not respond to any inquiries through that channel.
>
> For any business matters, please contact me directly at [email] or via [alternative channel]. Active client matters are unaffected.
>
> If you have received any communication from the compromised account since April 28, I would appreciate a screenshot for documentation. Thank you for your patience.

Same scenario. Two different voices. Both get the protective information across.

## What to avoid

- **Imposing a register on the user.** If the user normally writes casually, don't write them a corporate statement. Read the user's prior posts (or ask) to calibrate voice.
- **Excessive detail about the recovery process.** The audience doesn't care about Stage 3 of a 5-stage framework. They care about whether the message they got is real.
- **Speculation about the hijacker's identity.** Unless verified, "I think it was [someone]" is unfounded and potentially defamatory. Stick to "the account was compromised."
- **Promising a recovery timeline.** "I'll have this fixed by next week" — promises that might not be kept. Use language like "expecting" or "working toward."
- **Naming the platform's failures combatively.** This is for the audience, not the platform. Save the platform-failure framing for regulatory filings. Here, brief acknowledgment ("the platform's standard recovery is taking time") is enough.
- **Dragging the audience into the dispute.** Don't ask followers to flood the platform with reports. Don't ask them to publicly criticize the platform. The framework's leverage comes from regulatory channels, not from audience harassment of platform staff.

---

## Required structural elements

This document is the most flexible in the skill. The only structural requirement is clarity:

- **A factual statement of what happened, with date.**
- **A protective warning to the audience** if applicable (especially Scenarios A and E).
- **Alternative contact channels** the audience can use.
- **An optional ask for documentation** if useful for the case.

Length, tone, format are user-voice-dependent. Could be a tweet thread, a Threads post, an Instagram caption, a Patreon update, a blog post, an email blast — match the user's normal channels.

---

## Outline-first protocol (NOT REQUIRED for this document)

The community announcement is voice-driven, not fact-driven, and the stakes for hallucinated content are lower (it's going to the user's own audience, who will correct misstatements directly). Skip the outline step unless the user requests it.

The one quick check before writing: confirm with the user (1) which scenario applies, (2) what their normal voice looks like (ask them to share a recent post, or describe it), and (3) what alternative channels they want listed. Then draft.

---

## After the user posts

Walk them through:

1. **Post on every channel they have access to.** The compromised account itself can't be reached, but everywhere else (email list, other social platforms, Patreon, website) gets the announcement.
2. **Pin the announcement.** On Twitter/Threads/Bluesky/etc., pin the post so newcomers see it. On Instagram, save to highlights. On a website, link from the homepage.
3. **Save the post URL.** It's evidence — a dated public record of when the user lost access. Cite it in regulatory filings as "I publicly notified my audience on [date], URL [link]."
4. **Update once when resolved.** When the account is recovered, post a brief follow-up. Don't dwell. The audience moves on; the user should too.
